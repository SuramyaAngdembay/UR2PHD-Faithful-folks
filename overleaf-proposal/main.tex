\begin{abstract}
Chain-of-thought (CoT) faithfulness---whether a model's stated reasoning reflects the process that produced its answer---is increasingly central to oversight. We ask how far \emph{black-box} signals can go at detecting instance-level CoT unfaithfulness. On FaithCoT-Bench, we find that instance-level detection is saturated by simple answer-tracing and is strongly confounded by answer correctness. Three further signal families (premise-DAG structure, graph-targeted counterfactual interventions, and NLI step-support) fail to beat or augment answer-tracing. We localize the failure: detecting \emph{post-hoc rationalization on correct answers} is at chance for every black-box method. However, the information is internally decodable. We scale a white-box probe to a synthetic dataset of matched genuine vs. post-hoc traces across 6 models (Llama, Qwen, Gemma, DeepSeek), proving internal decodability is universal. Crucially, we show this synthetic "answer-first" proxy does not transfer to real organic post-hoc rationalization, offering a stern methodological caution to the interpretability community.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Large language models can generate fluent chain-of-thought (CoT) explanations that appear logical even when the stated reasoning did not actually produce the model's final answer. Recent work such as FaithCoT-Bench shows that detecting instance-level CoT unfaithfulness remains difficult \cite{shen2026faithcot}. 

Our work empirically audits the limits of black-box faithfulness detection. Instance-level detection of unfaithful CoT is saturated by answer-tracing and confounded by answer correctness. We rigorously test three further black-box signal families---premise-DAG structure, graph-targeted counterfactual interventions, and per-step NLI entailment support---and show none of them meaningfully augment simple answer-tracing.

\section{Contributions}

\textbf{C1. The Black-Box Frontier vs. Internal Probes.} No black-box signal detects post-hoc rationalization on correct answers above chance (AUROCs $\approx 0.50$). However, the information is internally decodable. We demonstrate that linear probes and L1-regularized Lasso probes on hidden states successfully detect unfaithfulness across 6 diverse models spanning 3 families from 2024 to 2026 (including Llama-3.1, Qwen-2.5, DeepSeek-R1, and Gemma-2) on a clean synthetic benchmark (AUROC 0.56--0.81, $p<0.025$).

\textbf{C2. The Proxy Bridge Failure.} We identify a critical methodological gap: while synthetic "answer-first" post-hoc constructions are internally coherent and highly detectable within their own distribution (peaking at early layers), they do \emph{not} transfer to real, naturally occurring post-hoc rationalization (which peaks at late layers). This cautions against the widespread use of prompt-induced rationalization as a proxy for organic unfaithfulness.

\textbf{C3. Metric Inversion.} The standard step-removal AUC metric widely used for faithfulness evaluation (e.g., \emph{soft\_faithfulness} in FaithCoT-Bench) actually anti-correlates with human faithfulness labels, exhibiting a mean difference of +0.139 (95\% CI: [+0.096, +0.182]), scoring higher for unfaithful traces. This serves as a concrete caution for a widely-used family of interpretability metrics.

\textbf{C4. Rigorous Negatives and Accuracy Confound.} Premise-DAG structure and graph-targeted counterfactual interventions do not beat answer-tracing. Answer correctness is the single strongest predictor of apparent unfaithfulness (0.697 AUROC)---quantifying the ``unfaithfulness as disguised accuracy'' confound. We note nuance where DAG centrality does show weak significance on mathematical domains (e.g., AQuA) where premise structure is explicit \cite{mukherjee2025parc}.

\section{Methodology and Evaluation}

\subsection{Datasets and Settings}
We evaluate across 4 domains (LogiQA, TruthfulQA, AQuA, HLE-Bio) utilizing FaithCoT-Bench for primary annotations \cite{shen2026faithcot}. We additionally construct a large-scale \emph{Synthetic Post-Hoc} dataset on mathematical reasoning (AQuA/GSM8K), eliciting paired genuine (reasoning-first) and post-hoc (answer-first) traces constrained to yield the exact same correct final answer.

\subsection{White-box Probing}
To test behavioral limits, we fit both dense linear probes (PCA to 50 dimensions, logistic regression/MLP) and sparse probes (L1-regularized Lasso regression directly on the 4096-dimensional stream) on last-token hidden states. This is applied to separate post-hoc rationalization from genuine reasoning. Significance is verified via 200-permutation selection-corrected tests.

\section{Conclusion and Next Steps}
Our findings rigorously map the limits of black-box CoT-faithfulness detection. Because behavioral metrics fail on the frontier of post-hoc rationalization on correct answers, future oversight tools must leverage mechanistic white-box methods or representation engineering to reliably audit reasoning integrity.
