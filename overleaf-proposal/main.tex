\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Research Problem}

Large language models can generate fluent chain-of-thought (CoT) explanations
that appear logical even when the stated reasoning did not support, or was not
responsible for, the model's final answer. This makes answer accuracy alone an
insufficient measure of trustworthy reasoning: a model may reach the correct
answer through an invalid path, rely on an unstated premise, ignore relevant
evidence, or construct a plausible explanation after already committing to an
answer. Recent work such as FaithCoT-Bench shows that detecting instance-level
CoT unfaithfulness remains difficult, particularly for knowledge-intensive
questions and convincing traces produced by stronger models
\cite{shen2026faithcot}. Our project will investigate whether explicitly
representing and verifying the premises behind individual reasoning steps can
improve the detection and localization of unfaithful CoT reasoning.

\subsection{Initial Approach}

We propose decomposing each CoT into atomic reasoning steps and representing the
dependencies between those steps as a premise graph. Each step will be checked
against the question, its declared earlier premises, and relevant external
evidence. The graph will also identify load-bearing premises for targeted
counterfactual tests: we will alter or remove a premise and observe whether the
downstream reasoning and final answer change appropriately. This approach is
motivated by Premise-Augmented Reasoning Chains, which demonstrates the value of
explicit premise links for reasoning-error identification
\cite{mukherjee2025parc}. We plan to evaluate the resulting detector on
FaithCoT-Bench/FINE-CoT using instance-level F1, step-level error localization,
counterfactual consistency, and comparisons with whole-trace and step-level
LLM-as-judge baselines. If time permits, we will explore whether answer
representations become detectable before the written CoT, following recent
work on post-hoc reasoning \cite{cox2026decoding}.
