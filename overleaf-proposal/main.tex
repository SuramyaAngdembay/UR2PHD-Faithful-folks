\begin{abstract}
Chain-of-thought (CoT) faithfulness---whether a model's stated reasoning reflects the process that produced its answer---is increasingly central to oversight. We ask how far \emph{black-box} signals can go at detecting instance-level CoT unfaithfulness. On FaithCoT-Bench (spanning 4 domains and 4 models) and GRACE, we find that instance-level detection is saturated by simple answer-tracing and is strongly confounded by answer correctness. Three further signal families (premise-DAG structure, graph-targeted counterfactual interventions, and NLI step-support) fail to beat or augment answer-tracing. We localize the failure: detecting \emph{post-hoc rationalization on correct answers} is at chance for every black-box method, because such rationalizations are locally coherent and yield stable answers. Furthermore, we show a widely-used step-removal faithfulness metric anti-correlates with human judgments. Our results bound black-box faithfulness detection and motivate mechanistic (white-box) approaches for the residual frontier.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Large language models can generate fluent chain-of-thought (CoT) explanations that appear logical even when the stated reasoning did not actually produce the model's final answer. Recent work such as FaithCoT-Bench shows that detecting instance-level CoT unfaithfulness remains difficult \cite{shen2026faithcot}. 

Our work empirically audits the limits of black-box faithfulness detection. Instance-level detection of unfaithful CoT is saturated by answer-tracing and confounded by answer correctness. We rigorously test three further black-box signal families---premise-DAG structure, graph-targeted counterfactual interventions, and per-step NLI entailment support---and show none of them meaningfully augment simple answer-tracing.

\section{Contributions}

\textbf{C1. The Black-Box Frontier vs. Internal Probes.} Across 4 domains and 4 models (n=270 in-regime traces), no black-box signal detects post-hoc rationalization on correct answers above chance. Answer-tracing, targeted interventions, NLI support, and DAG centrality all yield AUROCs hovering around 0.50. Mechanistically, post-hoc rationalizations are locally coherent and yield stable answers, rendering them externally indistinguishable. However, the information is internally decodable: a linear probe on hidden states detects post-hoc rationalization in Llama-3.1-8B (AUROC 0.71, permutation p=0.01) \cite{lanham2023measuring}.

\textbf{C2. Metric Inversion.} The standard step-removal AUC metric widely used for faithfulness evaluation (e.g., \emph{soft\_faithfulness} in FaithCoT-Bench) actually anti-correlates with human faithfulness labels, exhibiting a mean difference of +0.139 (95\% CI: [+0.096, +0.182]), scoring higher for unfaithful traces. This serves as a concrete caution for a widely-used family of interpretability metrics.

\textbf{C3. Rigorous Negatives and Accuracy Confound.} Premise-DAG structure and graph-targeted counterfactual interventions do not beat answer-tracing. Answer correctness is the single strongest predictor of apparent unfaithfulness (0.697 AUROC)---quantifying the ``unfaithfulness as disguised accuracy'' confound. We note nuance where DAG centrality does show weak significance on mathematical domains (e.g., AQuA) where premise structure is explicit \cite{mukherjee2025parc}.

\section{Methodology and Evaluation}

\subsection{Datasets and Settings}
We evaluate across 4 domains (LogiQA, TruthfulQA, AQuA, HLE-Bio) using 4 distinct language models (Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen-2.5-7B, etc.), comprising 1,304 extracted traces. We utilize FaithCoT-Bench for primary annotations and validate structural findings against the GRACE step-level benchmark \cite{shen2026faithcot, pham2026grace}.

\subsection{White-box Probing}
To test the behavioral accessibility limit, we fit a linear probe on the internal activations (last-token hidden states; PCA to 50 dimensions, logistic regression, 5-fold cross-validation) to separate post-hoc rationalization from genuine reasoning on correct answers. Statistical significance is verified via a 200-permutation selection-corrected test.

\section{Conclusion and Next Steps}
Our findings rigorously map the limits of black-box CoT-faithfulness detection. Because behavioral metrics fail on the frontier of post-hoc rationalization on correct answers, future oversight tools must leverage mechanistic white-box methods or representation engineering to reliably audit reasoning integrity.
