\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Research context and problem statement}

Large language models can generate fluent chain-of-thought (CoT) explanations
that appear logical even when the stated reasoning did not support, or was not
responsible for, the model's final answer \cite{lanham2023measuring}. This makes
answer accuracy alone an insufficient measure of trustworthy reasoning: a model
may reach the correct answer and then construct a plausible explanation after
already committing to it---\emph{post-hoc rationalization}. As CoT becomes an
oversight surface, where an auditor reads the reasoning to decide whether to
trust the answer, the practical question is instance-level: can \emph{this}
explanation be trusted? FaithCoT-Bench \cite{shen2026faithcot} formalizes this
problem with human annotations across four domains and four models, and shows
that detection remains difficult. Adjacent lines of work verify reasoning chains
structurally, via premise graphs and logical-consistency checks
\cite{mukherjee2025parc, fang2025gov, feng2025vericot}, or evaluate step-level
error localization \cite{zheng2024processbench, jacovi2024reveal}---these target
reasoning \emph{correctness}, whereas our question is whether the stated
reasoning is the \emph{actual cause} of the answer.

Our previous proposal set out to \emph{build} a premise-grounded detector,
combining PARC-style premise graphs \cite{mukherjee2025parc} with graph-targeted
counterfactual interventions. We implemented and evaluated that method first,
and its rigorously-established failure (below) reshaped the project. The revised
project asks three questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1:} Where exactly does black-box (behavioral) faithfulness
          detection fail, and why?
    \item \textbf{RQ2:} Where behavior is uninformative, is the distinguishing
          information still present in the model's internal activations
          \cite{cox2026decoding}?
    \item \textbf{RQ3:} Can this regime be studied at scale with synthetically
          constructed data, or does constructed unfaithfulness differ from the
          organic kind?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Approach and preliminary findings}

\textbf{Black-box signals fail on a specific, identifiable frontier (RQ1).}
We implemented four families of behavioral detectors on FaithCoT-Bench---answer-tracing
(step-removal sensitivity), premise-graph structure, targeted counterfactual
interventions (with an LLM premise extractor validated at 0.82 recall against gold
annotations, so the test is not an extraction artifact), and per-step NLI
support---and scored them against the \emph{human} labels with bootstrap confidence
intervals. Two diagnoses emerged. First, answer correctness alone is the strongest
single ``unfaithfulness'' signal (AUROC 0.696): unfaithful traces in this benchmark
are disproportionately \emph{correct} ones, so black-box detection largely reduces to
accuracy prediction. Second, the benchmark's own step-removal metric
\emph{anti-correlates} with human faithfulness (unfaithful traces score higher by
$+0.139$, 95\% CI $[+0.096,+0.182]$); used as intended it performs significantly
\emph{below} chance (AUROC 0.349). The controlled test follows: restrict to traces
where the answer is correct, and ask whether anything separates genuine reasoning
from post-hoc rationalization. Table~\ref{tab:frontier} shows the result---every
black-box signal is at chance. We call this the \emph{black-box frontier}.

\textbf{The information is partially inside the model (RQ2).} On the same
human-annotated regime, simple linear probes over hidden activations (PCA-50 +
logistic regression, with selection-corrected permutation tests) succeed where
behavior fails---in some models. In Llama-3.1-8B the probe reaches held-out AUROC
0.70 and generalizes across domains (0.60--0.71 leave-one-domain-out); in
Qwen-2.5-7B it is weak and not significant ($n=126$), so the effect is
model-dependent. A first causal check points the same way: steering activations
along the probe direction flips answers 2--3$\times$ more than a norm-matched
random direction (0.22 vs.\ 0.08), though we treat this as weak, suggestive
evidence.

\textbf{Scaling with constructed data works---but the construction is not the
real thing (RQ3).} Because annotated post-hoc traces are scarce, we built matched
pairs on math datasets (AQuA, GSM8K): the same model either solves a problem
step-by-step (kept only when correct; ``genuine'') or is handed the gold answer
and asked to justify it (``post-hoc''). Both end at the same correct answer, so
only the causal role of the reasoning differs. Across seven open models spanning
three families and 2024--2026 (Table~\ref{tab:synth}), the internal signal is
permutation-significant in every model, strongest in standard instruct models and
weakest in reasoning-distilled ones, and it is not explained by surface style
(the black-box baseline stays near chance for five of seven). However, probes
trained on this construction do \emph{not} transfer to the real annotated traces,
or vice versa (Table~\ref{tab:bridge}): the two signals peak at different network
depths and cross-transfer is near chance. Preliminarily, prompt-induced
``answer-first'' reasoning is not a representational proxy for organic post-hoc
rationalization---a caution for a common shortcut in the field, and the
motivation for RQ3's proposed data-collection work.

\section{Evaluation and Implementation Plan}

\subsection{Evaluation Plan}

\textbf{Datasets.} FaithCoT-Bench remains the primary benchmark (the only
instance-level CoT-faithfulness dataset with expert annotations)
\cite{shen2026faithcot}, evaluated strictly against its human labels---we showed
that evaluating against its derived metric is near-circular. For external
validity we will replicate on the GRACE step-level faithfulness benchmark
\cite{pham2026grace} when its full evaluation set ($\sim$437 traces) is released
(authors contacted; our run on the 40-example public sample is directionally
consistent but underpowered). The constructed genuine/post-hoc dataset (AQuA,
GSM8K) supports the scaling work; per the bridge result, conclusions about
\emph{organic} unfaithfulness will not be drawn from constructed data alone.

\textbf{Statistical standards.} Detection claims use AUROC with 2{,}000-resample
bootstrap 95\% CIs; probe claims use held-out splits plus selection-corrected
permutation tests (max-over-layers null); causal claims must beat norm-matched
random-direction controls, with effect sizes reported regardless of outcome.
Negative results are reported as findings. Extraction-dependent methods are
interpreted only after validating the extractor against gold annotations.

\textbf{Remaining work.} In priority order:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Consolidation (RQ1):} finalize the analysis manuscript
          (maintained separately from this proposal) and port it to the
          ACL/BlackboxNLP template; target venue BlackboxNLP @ EMNLP 2026.
    \item \textbf{Causal strengthening (RQ2):} activation-patching/ablation along
          the probe direction with pre-registered success criteria, and a study of
          why decodability is model-dependent (reasoning-distilled models are
          consistently harder to read).
    \item \textbf{Organic data pilot (RQ3):} sample many traces per question at
          temperature, nominate candidate post-hoc traces via answer-precommitment
          probes and consistency checks, and human-verify a subset---a small but
          organic evaluation set for the frontier.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Timeline}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{July 2026:} manuscript polish; advisor review; ACL-template port.
    \item \textbf{August 2026:} BlackboxNLP @ EMNLP 2026 submission (CFP deadline
          expected August; to be verified); begin activation-patching experiments.
    \item \textbf{September--October 2026:} GRACE replication upon release;
          organic-data pilot. \emph{Stretch (hardware-gated):} extend the roster to
          $\geq$16\,GB-class models (Gemma-4-31B, DeepSeek-V4).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Risks.} GRACE may not release in time (the paper stands on FaithCoT-Bench
alone); the organic pilot may yield few verified traces (even a small set, or a
null, is informative for the bridge question); compute caps model scale (the
7-model roster already spans families and generations).

\section*{Revisions Since the Previous Submission}

Following advisor guidance to validate the core assumption before further
method-building, and incorporating feedback on evaluation rigor:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The previously proposed method was implemented, evaluated---and
          it failed.} Premise-graph structure and targeted interventions do not
          beat simple answer-tracing against human labels. We report this as a
          rigorous negative result and re-scoped the project from method-building
          to an analysis of the limits of black-box faithfulness detection
          (RQ1--RQ3); the title changed accordingly.
    \item \textbf{Ground truth changed from derived metrics to human annotations},
          after finding the benchmark's own metric is near-circular to evaluate
          against and anti-correlates with human judgments.
    \item \textbf{Scale and rigor were upgraded:} from a 2-domain pilot to 4
          domains $\times$ 4 models with bootstrap CIs, selection-corrected
          permutation tests, and gold-validated premise extraction.
    \item \textbf{New preliminary work since the last submission:} the frontier
          localization (Table~\ref{tab:frontier}); white-box probing with a first
          causal check; the seven-model constructed-data study
          (Table~\ref{tab:synth}); and the real$\leftrightarrow$synthetic transfer
          failure (Table~\ref{tab:bridge}), now a dedicated work package.
    \item \textbf{Structure:} restored to the course proposal format, with
          preliminary findings in tables, an explicit evaluation plan with failure
          criteria, a timeline with risks, and a named target venue. The full
          paper manuscript is maintained in a separate document.
\end{enumerate}
