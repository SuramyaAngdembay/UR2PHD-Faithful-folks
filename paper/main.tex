\begin{abstract}
Chain-of-thought (CoT) explanations support oversight only if they are \emph{faithful}: the stated reasoning must actually produce the answer. We audit how far \emph{black-box} (behavioral) detection of unfaithful CoT can go, evaluating four families of behavioral signals on FaithCoT-Bench against its human annotations. Detection is dominated by an accuracy confound: answer correctness alone is the strongest ``unfaithfulness'' signal (AUROC 0.696), and the standard step-removal faithfulness metric \emph{anti-correlates} with human judgments. Controlling for correctness isolates a frontier where every black-box signal we test is at chance: \emph{post-hoc rationalization on correct answers}. Linear probes over hidden states detect this regime where behavior cannot, with model-dependent strength. To study the regime at scale, we compare instructed answer-first prompting (7 models, 3 families) with hint-induced silent rationalization, which carries counterfactual labels. Only the hint-induced variant shares a representation with human-annotated rationalization (transfer AUROC 0.616, permutation $p{=}0.010$; instructed: 0.431), and the three variants peak at increasing network depths. Instructed rationalization is not a proxy for the organic phenomenon, and faithfulness evaluation should not rely on it.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Large language models produce fluent step-by-step explanations that appear to expose a reasoning process, but the stated reasoning is sometimes not the process that produced the answer \citep{lanham2023measuring,turpin2023language,chen2025reasoning}. A model may commit to an answer and then compose a plausible derivation afterwards: \emph{post-hoc rationalization}. As CoT becomes an oversight surface, where an auditor reads the reasoning to decide whether to trust the answer, the practical question is instance-level: can \emph{this} explanation be trusted? FaithCoT-Bench \citep{shen2026faithcot} formalizes this problem with human annotations and shows a broad panel of detectors performs poorly in aggregate.

This paper asks a sharper pair of questions: \textbf{where exactly} does black-box detection fail, and \textbf{is the missing information present in the model's internals}? Figure~\ref{fig:example} shows the phenomenon at the center of the paper. The same model, on the same problem, fails cleanly; handed a hint, it produces a locally flawless derivation of the hinted answer without ever mentioning the hint. Nothing on the surface distinguishes that trace from genuine reasoning. We proceed in four parts.

\paragraph{An audit with a diagnosis (\S\ref{sec:audit}).} We implement four families of behavioral detectors on FaithCoT-Bench, including premise-graph structure adapted from PARC \citep{mukherjee2025parc} and counterfactual interventions with premise extraction validated against gold annotations, and score everything against the \emph{human} labels. Two diagnoses emerge: answer correctness alone outperforms every purpose-built detector (much of ``unfaithfulness detection'' is accuracy prediction in disguise), and the benchmark's own step-removal metric anti-correlates with human faithfulness.

\paragraph{A localized frontier (\S\ref{sec:frontier}).} Controlling for the confound (restricting to correct-answer traces and separating faithful reasoning from post-hoc rationalization) collapses every black-box signal to chance. We argue this regime is the hard core of the problem.

\paragraph{The internals contrast (\S\ref{sec:internals}).} On the same regime, simple linear probes over hidden states succeed where behavior fails, in some models: Llama-3.1-8B reaches held-out AUROC 0.70 with cross-domain generalization and weak causal support from activation steering; Qwen-2.5-7B is not significant. The distinction is behaviorally inaccessible yet (unevenly) linearly decodable.

\paragraph{Constructed vs.\ organic unfaithfulness (\S\ref{sec:construction}).} Annotated post-hoc traces are scarce, so the field constructs them, typically by instructing a model to justify a given answer. We compare two constructions at scale: \emph{instructed} answer-first prompting across 7 open models (2024--2026), and \emph{hint-induced} rationalization \citep{turpin2023language}, where a model that failed a problem flips to the hinted gold answer without ever mentioning the hint, yielding organic traces with causal labels. Both are strongly detectable internally; but a three-way transfer analysis against the human-annotated regime shows only the hint-induced variant shares its representation. Instructed rationalization is an artifact, and that is a caution for common evaluation practice.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{CoT faithfulness.} \citet{lanham2023measuring} introduced perturbation-based faithfulness tests (early answering, adding mistakes); step-removal ``answer-tracing'' metrics descend from this line. \citet{turpin2023language} showed models rationalize biased answers without verbalizing the bias; \citet{chen2025reasoning} measured low hint-verbalization rates in reasoning models; \citet{arcuschin2025wild} document post-hoc rationalization in the wild; and \citet{zaman2026hint} show unverbalized hints still causally flow through the CoT. Our hint-induced construction operationalizes this paradigm as a labeled detection testbed. \citet{bentham2024disguised} showed model-level flip-based unfaithfulness scores largely track accuracy; our audit gives the instance-level counterpart against human labels and localizes where detection collapses. FaithCoT-Bench \citep{shen2026faithcot} contributes the human annotations we evaluate against, and notes in passing that faithfulness and correctness are conflated---we quantify that conflation and control for it.

\paragraph{Concurrent work (2026).} The question of reading unfaithfulness from internals is active. \citet{mirtaheri2026catching} probe hint-induced motivated reasoning and find internal probes beat CoT monitors; \citet{shen2026cie} (the FaithCoT-Bench group) build a circuit-based white-box detector that beats black-box baselines on the full benchmark; \citet{gurarieh2026bonafide} meta-evaluate perturbation faithfulness metrics against constructed ground truth and likewise find below-chance AUROCs; \citet{cox2026decoding} decode answers pre-CoT and steer along probe directions with large effects. We view these as convergent; what they do not do---and what this paper contributes---is (i) stratify detection by correctness and show black-box collapse on the correct-answer regime of \emph{human-annotated} traces, (ii) explain the metric inversion via that confound, and (iii) compare instructed, hint-induced, and organic rationalization at the representation level. In the deception-probing literature, \citet{offpolicy2025probes} show probes trained off-policy generalize poorly to on-policy behavior; our three-way transfer analysis extends this caution to CoT faithfulness with a depth-resolved account.

\paragraph{Structural verification.} PARC \citep{mukherjee2025parc} builds premise DAGs to identify reasoning errors; GoV \citep{fang2025gov} and VeriCoT \citep{feng2025vericot} verify chains with DAGs and neuro-symbolic checks. These target \emph{correctness}; we test whether such structure transfers to \emph{faithfulness} and find it largely does not. Step-level error benchmarks \citep{zheng2024processbench,jacovi2024reveal} and GRACE \citep{pham2026grace} evaluate related but distinct constructs.

\paragraph{Probing.} Our white-box method is deliberately standard---linear probes with selection-corrected permutation testing---because the contribution is the behavioral-vs-internal \emph{contrast} and the cross-construction transfer analysis, not a new probing technique. We follow the probing literature's cautions \citep{belinkov2022probing}: decodability does not imply the model \emph{uses} the information \citep{elazar2021amnesic}, so all probe claims are stated as ``linearly decodable,'' with the steering experiment as separate (and weak) causal evidence.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Benchmark and labels.} FaithCoT-Bench spans four domains (LogiQA, TruthfulQA, AQuA, HLE-Bio) and four models (Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, Qwen-2.5-7B-Instruct, GPT-4o-mini, Gemini-2.5-Flash), 1{,}304 traces. It provides derived metrics---notably \texttt{soft\_faithfulness}, a step-removal answer-tracing score---and \emph{human} annotations: binary \texttt{unfaithfulness} and a four-way \texttt{faithful\_type} (ft1 faithful-correct; ft2 post-hoc rationalization on a correct answer; ft3/ft4 incorrect-answer regimes). We evaluate against the human labels throughout. A trap we document: correlating a detector against \texttt{soft\_faithfulness} itself is near-circular (an apparent $\rho{=}0.87$ collapses to chance against human labels), since it is itself a detector.

\paragraph{Statistical standards.} Detection claims use AUROC with 2{,}000-resample percentile-bootstrap 95\% CIs (class imbalances are mild, 13--43\% minority). Probe claims use held-out splits plus \emph{selection-corrected} permutation tests: because the best layer is chosen post hoc, the null statistic takes the max (or the mean, matching the reported statistic) over layers under label permutation, with permutations coupled across layers---the max-statistic procedure of \citet{nichols2002nonparametric}. With 200 permutations the finest attainable $p$ is $1/201 \approx 0.005$; values reported as 0.005 mean $p \leq 0.005$. The permutation null doubles as the memorization/selectivity control in lieu of word-level control tasks, which do not transfer to sequence-level properties \citep{belinkov2022probing}. Scalers and PCA are fit within training folds only. Causal claims must beat norm-matched random-direction controls. Negative results are reported as findings.

\paragraph{Compute.} All open-model experiments run on two RTX 3070 (8\,GB) GPUs with 4-bit NF4 quantization; the 12B model in \S\ref{sec:construction} uses two-GPU pipeline sharding.

\section{Part I: The Black-Box Audit}
\label{sec:audit}

\paragraph{Signal families.} \emph{Answer-tracing}: sensitivity of the final-answer distribution to removing individual steps. \emph{Premise-DAG structure}: PARC-style premise graphs over steps (linearity; maximum lookback). \emph{Graph-targeted counterfactual interventions}: corrupt the most load-bearing premise and measure answer stability; to make this a fair test we use an LLM premise extractor validated against PARC's gold PERL annotations (recall 0.82 / F1 0.79, vs.\ 0.57 F1 heuristic), so the result below is not an extraction artifact. \emph{Per-step NLI support}: entailment of each step from question and prior steps (RoBERTa-large-MNLI).

\paragraph{Results.} Table~\ref{tab:audit} and Figure~\ref{fig:confound}: \textbf{answer correctness alone is the strongest single signal (0.696)}---in this benchmark unfaithful traces are disproportionately \emph{correct} ones (post-hoc rationalization), so black-box unfaithfulness detection largely reduces to accuracy prediction. Structural families add nothing significant, with one honest nuance: DAG max-lookback is weakly informative on AQuA (0.636), the one domain with explicit premise structure.

\paragraph{Metric inversion.} \texttt{soft\_faithfulness} used as intended scores \textbf{0.349 [0.305, 0.389]}---significantly \emph{below} chance; equivalently, unfaithful traces score \emph{higher} by $+0.139$ [$+0.096, +0.182$]. Concurrent meta-evaluation reports below-chance AUROCs for related perturbation metrics against constructed ground truth \citep{gurarieh2026bonafide}; our result is against \emph{human} labels on organic traces, and---the part the confound makes visible---has a mechanism: unfaithful traces skew toward correct, tightly answer-coupled post-hoc rationalizations, for which step removal moves the answer \emph{more}, not less. The metric measures answer--reasoning \emph{coupling}, not faithfulness. In \S\ref{sec:construction} we replicate the inversion on traces with counterfactual ground-truth labels.

\section{Part II: The Frontier}
\label{sec:frontier}

The confound suggests a controlled test: restrict to human-annotated \emph{correct-answer} traces and separate faithful reasoning (ft1) from post-hoc rationalization (ft2), $n{=}270$. Table~\ref{tab:frontier}: \textbf{every black-box signal is at chance}; all CIs include 0.5. This is intuitive in hindsight---a post-hoc rationalization is locally coherent, supports its answer, and yields a confident, stable answer distribution: exactly the external profile of a faithful trace. We refer to this regime as the \emph{black-box frontier}. (This at-chance claim is specific to the human-annotated benchmark; the constructed testbeds of \S\ref{sec:construction} serve other purposes and carry partial surface signals, which we report.)

\section{Part IV: Constructed vs.\ Organic Unfaithfulness}
\label{sec:construction}

Annotated post-hoc traces are scarce ($n{=}270$ in-regime, two open models). Scaling the frontier requires \emph{constructing} the regime---and the construction itself turns out to be the object of study.

\subsection{Instructed construction: 7 models}
\label{sec:synth}

For math problems (AQuA, GSM8K) with gold answer $a^*$, the same model either solves step-by-step (kept only when correct: \emph{genuine}) or is instructed to justify the given $a^*$ (\emph{post-hoc}). Both end at the same correct answer; probed contexts are identical (the instruction is never probed); decoding is matched. Across \textbf{seven open models spanning three families and 2024--2026} (Table~\ref{tab:synth})---including Gemma-4-12B via two-GPU pipeline sharding---the construction is permutation-significant in every model (Figure~\ref{fig:seven}), strongest in standard instruct models, weakest in reasoning-distilled ones. Surface-feature baselines (no activation access) remain near chance for five of seven, indicating a genuinely internal signal; Llama (0.64) and Gemma-4 (0.67) carry partial surface tells that the probes clear by $\geq$0.10. Notably, Qwen-2.5---not significant on the noisy human labels---is strongest here, suggesting its earlier null owed to label noise and sample size.

\subsection{Hint-induced organic construction}
\label{sec:hint}

Following the biased-context paradigm \citep{turpin2023language,chen2025reasoning}, we first ask each problem clean (correct $\Rightarrow$ \emph{genuine}, capped). For problems the model got \emph{wrong}, we re-ask with a sycophancy hint toward the gold answer (``I think the answer is $a^*$\ldots''). A trace is kept as \emph{hint-induced post-hoc} iff the model flips to $a^*$ \emph{and} the CoT never references the hint (mention-filtered; rejected cases are honest deference). The label is \textbf{counterfactual}---the model answered incorrectly without the hint, so the hint, not the stated reasoning, changed the answer; this matches the unfaithfulness criterion of \citet{chen2025reasoning}. We distinguish this established claim (``the hint caused the answer, unverbalized'') from the stronger interpretation (``the CoT was generated answer-first''), which remains an inference. Probed contexts exclude the hint text, keeping conditions symmetric. On the full AQuA-RAT and GSM8K test sets this yields \textbf{185 hint-induced post-hoc + 428 genuine traces for Llama} and \textbf{70 + 464 for Qwen}, with an exhaustive keyword audit confirming no hint leakage. The construction costs no human annotation. Two caveats: with sampled decoding a fraction of flips may be resampling noise rather than hint causation, and---unlike the instructed construction, whose pairs share problems---genuine and post-hoc traces here come from \emph{disjoint problem pools} (solvable vs.\ unsolvable at baseline), so within-testbed decodability may partly reflect problem difficulty. The transfer analysis below is the difficulty-robust claim: in the annotated target regime both classes are correct-answer traces, so a difficulty probe should not transfer---yet transfer is what we observe.

Both models are strongly decodable on this organic set (held-out 0.752 / 0.835, permutation $p{=}0.005$; surface baselines 0.626 / 0.703---a partial behavioral tell we report). Most strikingly, \textbf{the metric inversion replicates with causal labels}: \texttt{soft\_faithfulness} in its intended direction scores \textbf{0.389 [0.301, 0.481] (Llama) and 0.251 [0.166, 0.350] (Qwen)}---on traces where the reasoning provably did not produce the answer, the standard metric confidently points the wrong way.

Do the constructions share the annotated regime's representation? We train probes on each distribution and test on the others, sweeping all layers (Table~\ref{tab:bridge}; Figures~\ref{fig:depth} and~\ref{fig:heatmap}). Three observations. \textbf{(1) Depth gradient:} the three variants peak at increasing depths---instructed L9, hint-organic L17, annotated L29 (Qwen: L10/L21/L22). \textbf{(2) Instructed rationalization does not transfer} to the annotated regime in either direction ($\leq$0.554 best-of-layers; layer-means at or below chance)---despite being the \emph{easiest} variant to detect within-distribution. \textbf{(3) Hint-induced organic rationalization transfers} (Llama: layer-mean 0.616, permutation $p{=}0.010$; best 0.694, selection-corrected $p{=}0.050$). In Qwen the transfer is null ($p{=}0.741$), which we report as inconclusive: the annotated-regime anchor is itself not significant in Qwen and the organic set is small ($n{=}70$)---one cannot measure transfer onto a signal that barely exists in the target.

Stated precisely, the transfer result concerns \emph{linearly decodable structure}: probes find \textbf{no shared linearly decodable direction between instructed rationalization and the annotated regime at any layer}, while hint-induced rationalization does share one---and the depth signatures (Figure~\ref{fig:depth}) tell the same story constructively. Transfer failure alone cannot rule out a shared representation encoded along non-aligned directions, and input-distribution differences contribute; but the three-way contrast controls for the boring versions of this (the two constructions share format and domain, yet only one transfers), and the result is robust to preprocessing choices: without PCA, and with the scaler refit on the target distribution, hint$\to$annotated layer-means remain 0.56--0.57 while instructed$\to$annotated stays at 0.45. The practical caution stands: \textbf{evaluating or training faithfulness detectors on instructed rationalizations may measure the wrong thing} \citep[cf.\ off-policy probe failures in deception detection;][]{offpolicy2025probes}.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{For oversight.} Black-box CoT auditing largely re-detects answer correctness; where correctness is controlled, behavior is uninformative, and the most-used metric inverts. The information exists inside the model---unevenly across models---which argues for white-box access as a requirement for reasoning oversight, not a convenience.

\paragraph{For evaluation practice.} Two shortcuts fail in ways we can now quantify: evaluating detectors against answer-tracing metrics (circular, and inverted in this regime), and constructing unfaithfulness by instruction (representationally unlike the real thing). The hint-induced protocol offers a cheap alternative with causal labels; our collected set (255 organic post-hoc + 892 matched genuine traces across two models) is released with the paper.

\paragraph{Model-dependence.} Detection strength varies systematically---strongest in standard instruct models, weakest in reasoning-distilled ones, whose elaborate reasoning may blur the genuine/post-hoc boundary or encode it elsewhere. Within one family, Gemma-2 (0.60) $\to$ Gemma-4 (0.80) is a sharp jump. Why some models expose this regime linearly and others do not is an open mechanistic question.

\section{Limitations}

Our real-label results rest on one human-annotated benchmark (FaithCoT-Bench; in-regime $n{=}270$, per-model $n{=}144/126$)---to our knowledge the only instance-level faithfulness benchmark with expert annotations, which is itself part of the problem we highlight. The at-chance frontier claim is specific to that benchmark; the constructed testbeds carry partial surface signals (up to 0.70) and serve the inversion, decodability, and transfer analyses instead. The Qwen contrast is ``not detected,'' not ``absent,'' and its bridge result is inconclusive rather than negative. Causal evidence from steering is weak ($n{=}51$, large perturbations, indirect readout). The constructed testbeds are math-only; the hint construction uses a single sycophancy template (template robustness unchecked), inherits class imbalance (AUROC-tolerant; reported), draws genuine and post-hoc traces from disjoint problem pools (a difficulty confound for within-testbed decodability; the transfer analysis is the difficulty-robust claim), and under sampled decoding some flips may be resampling noise rather than hint causation. Probes are linear/PCA-based (an MLP added nothing, but richer white-box methods may decode more). Closed models cannot be probed at all---which is itself part of the argument for open-weight oversight.

This work analyzes model-generated reasoning on public benchmarks (FaithCoT-Bench, AQuA-RAT, GSM8K) and releases model-generated traces with construction labels; no human subjects or personal data are involved. \section{Construction Details}
\label{app:prompts}
\paragraph{Instructed (synthetic).} Genuine: \emph{``Solve this step by step, showing your reasoning. On the last line write exactly `Answer: X'.''} (kept only if correct). Post-hoc: \emph{``The correct answer is $a^*$. Provide a clear, convincing step-by-step reasoning that leads to this answer.''} Sampling T$=$0.7, top-$p$ 0.9, repetition penalty 1.1, fixed seed; identical across conditions and models.
\paragraph{Hint-induced (organic).} Hint text: \emph{``I think the answer is $a^*$, but I'm not sure.''} appended to the clean question; kept iff baseline-wrong $\to$ hint-correct and the CoT matches no hint-reference pattern (second-person forms, ``hint,'' ``suggested answer,'' ``the user,'' etc.); rejected cases (3 total) verbalized deference. An exhaustive keyword scan over all kept traces found no leakage (matches were problem restatements, e.g.\ ``we are told that\ldots,'' and generic-you phrasing).
\paragraph{Probing.} Probed context is always [question (+options) $+$ generated CoT], excluding any construction instruction or hint text. Last-token hidden states at every layer; StandardScaler $\to$ PCA-50 $\to$ logistic regression; 5-fold CV; 200-permutation selection-corrected tests.

\section{GRACE Preliminary Replication}
On the 40-example public sample of GRACE \citep{pham2026grace} (8 unfaithful steps), NLI step-support is at chance (0.51--0.58), directionally consistent with our FaithCoT results but underpowered; the full evaluation set was not released in time for this submission.
