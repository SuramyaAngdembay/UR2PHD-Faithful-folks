\begin{abstract}
Chain-of-thought (CoT) explanations support oversight only if they are \emph{faithful}: the stated reasoning must actually produce the answer. We audit \emph{black-box} (behavioral) detection of unfaithful CoT on FaithCoT-Bench against its human annotations, and find that answer correctness structures the problem at every level. First, correctness alone is the strongest ``unfaithfulness'' signal (AUROC 0.696): 69\% of annotated unfaithfulness sits on \emph{incorrect} answers. Second, detection splits into two regimes. On correct answers, behavioral signals moderately separate faithful reasoning from post-hoc rationalization (0.63--0.67); on incorrect answers---where most unfaithfulness lives---every signal we test is at chance. Third, the standard step-removal faithfulness metric \emph{anti-correlates} with the human labels; the inversion reproduces on the benchmark's own released scores and on traces with counterfactual ground-truth labels. Fourth, linear probes over hidden states decode the behaviorally blind regime in Llama-3.1-8B and the correct-answer regime in Qwen-2.5-7B, while cross-regime probe transfer is not detected: we find no shared, positively aligned linear direction between the regimes. Finally, comparing instructed answer-first construction (7 models, 3 families) with hint-induced unverbalized answer flips, only the hint-induced variant shares linearly decodable structure with annotated unfaithfulness. In the course of the audit we also identify, verify, and correct a label-semantics discrepancy between the benchmark's documentation and its released data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Large language models produce fluent step-by-step explanations that appear to expose a reasoning process, but the stated reasoning is sometimes not the process that produced the answer \citep{lanham2023measuring,turpin2023language,chen2025reasoning}. As CoT becomes an oversight surface, where an auditor reads the reasoning to decide whether to trust the answer, the practical question is instance-level: can \emph{this} explanation be trusted? FaithCoT-Bench \citep{shen2026faithcot} formalizes this problem with human annotations and shows a broad panel of detectors performs poorly in aggregate.

This paper asks \textbf{what behavioral detection actually detects}, and finds that the answer, at every level of the analysis, is \textbf{answer correctness}. Figure~\ref{fig:example} shows the phenomenon that motivates the paper: the same model, on the same problem, fails cleanly; handed a hint, it produces a locally flawless derivation of the hinted answer without ever mentioning the hint. Nothing on the surface distinguishes that trace from genuine reasoning. We proceed in four parts.

\paragraph{An audit with a diagnosis (\S\ref{sec:audit}).} We implement four families of behavioral detectors on FaithCoT-Bench, including premise-graph structure adapted from PARC \citep{mukherjee2025parc} and counterfactual interventions with premise extraction validated against gold annotations, and score everything against the \emph{human} labels. Answer correctness alone outperforms every purpose-built detector, because unfaithfulness concentrates on incorrect answers: much of ``unfaithfulness detection'' is accuracy prediction in disguise. En route we document a label-semantics discrepancy between the benchmark's documentation and its released data (\S\ref{sec:labels}), which we verify three independent ways and correct throughout.

\paragraph{Two regimes (\S\ref{sec:regimes}).} Stratifying by correctness splits detection into two regimes with opposite characters. On \emph{correct} answers ($n{=}363$), behavioral signals moderately separate faithful reasoning from post-hoc rationalization (0.63--0.67), and the benchmark's own step-removal metric points confidently the wrong way. On \emph{incorrect} answers ($n{=}270$)---honest error versus unfaithful error, the regime holding 69\% of the benchmark's annotated unfaithfulness---every black-box signal we test collapses to chance.

\paragraph{The internals contrast (\S\ref{sec:internals}).} Linear probes over hidden states decode each regime in a different model: the behaviorally blind incorrect-answer regime in Llama-3.1-8B (nested held-out AUROC 0.67, selection-corrected permutation $p{\leq}0.03$) and the correct-answer regime in Qwen-2.5-7B ($p{=}0.014$). Probes trained on one regime do not transfer to the other in either model: no shared, positively aligned linear direction is detected across regimes.

\paragraph{Constructed vs.\ organic unfaithfulness (\S\ref{sec:construction}).} Annotated unfaithful traces are scarce, so the field constructs them, typically by instructing a model to justify a given answer. We compare two constructions at scale: \emph{instructed} answer-first prompting across 7 open models (2024--2026), and \emph{hint-induced} rationalization \citep{turpin2023language}, where a model that failed a problem flips to the hinted gold answer without ever mentioning the hint. Both are strongly detectable internally; but in transfer against the annotated data, only the hint-induced variant shares linearly decodable structure with annotated unfaithfulness---and, unexpectedly, with the \emph{incorrect}-answer regime rather than the correct-answer regime its traces superficially resemble. Instructed rationalization transfers to neither: a caution for common evaluation practice.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{CoT faithfulness.} \citet{lanham2023measuring} introduced perturbation-based faithfulness tests (early answering, adding mistakes); step-removal ``answer-tracing'' metrics descend from this line. \citet{turpin2023language} showed models rationalize biased answers without verbalizing the bias; \citet{chen2025reasoning} measured low hint-verbalization rates in reasoning models; \citet{arcuschin2025wild} document post-hoc rationalization in the wild; and \citet{zaman2026hint} show unverbalized hints still causally flow through the CoT. Our hint-induced construction operationalizes this paradigm as a labeled detection testbed. \citet{bentham2024disguised} showed model-level flip-based unfaithfulness scores largely track accuracy; our audit gives the instance-level counterpart against human labels, and shows the accuracy coupling runs deeper: it partitions detection into regimes with opposite behavioral profiles. FaithCoT-Bench \citep{shen2026faithcot} contributes the human annotations we evaluate against, and notes in passing that faithfulness and correctness are conflated---we quantify that conflation, control for it, and correct a label-semantics discrepancy in the benchmark's release (\S\ref{sec:labels}).

\paragraph{Concurrent work (2026).} The question of reading unfaithfulness from internals is active. \citet{mirtaheri2026catching} probe hint-induced motivated reasoning and find internal probes beat CoT monitors; \citet{shen2026cie} (the FaithCoT-Bench group) build a circuit-based white-box detector that beats black-box baselines on the full benchmark; \citet{gurarieh2026bonafide} meta-evaluate perturbation faithfulness metrics against constructed ground truth and likewise find below-chance AUROCs; \citet{cox2026decoding} decode answers pre-CoT and steer along probe directions with large effects. We view these as convergent; what they do not do---and what this paper contributes---is (i) stratify \emph{human-annotated} detection by answer correctness and show that behavioral detection splits into a moderately detectable correct-answer regime and a blind incorrect-answer regime holding most of the unfaithfulness, (ii) explain the metric inversion via that structure and reproduce it on the benchmark's own scores and on counterfactually labeled traces, and (iii) compare instructed, hint-induced, and organic unfaithfulness at the representation level, including across regimes. In the deception-probing literature, \citet{offpolicy2025probes} show probes trained off-policy generalize poorly to on-policy behavior; our transfer analysis extends this caution to CoT faithfulness.

\paragraph{Structural verification.} PARC \citep{mukherjee2025parc} builds premise DAGs to identify reasoning errors; GoV \citep{fang2025gov} and VeriCoT \citep{feng2025vericot} verify chains with DAGs and neuro-symbolic checks. These target \emph{correctness}; we test whether such structure transfers to \emph{faithfulness} and find it largely does not. Step-level error benchmarks \citep{zheng2024processbench,jacovi2024reveal} and GRACE \citep{pham2026grace} evaluate related but distinct constructs.

\paragraph{Probing.} Our white-box method is deliberately standard---linear probes with selection-corrected permutation testing---because the contribution is the behavioral-vs-internal \emph{contrast} and the cross-regime, cross-construction transfer analysis, not a new probing technique. We follow the probing literature's cautions \citep{belinkov2022probing}: decodability does not imply the model \emph{uses} the information \citep{elazar2021amnesic}, so all probe claims are stated as ``linearly decodable,'' with the steering experiment as separate (and weak) causal evidence.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Benchmark.} FaithCoT-Bench spans four domains (LogiQA, TruthfulQA, AQuA, HLE-Bio) and four models (Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, Qwen-2.5-7B-Instruct, GPT-4o-mini, Gemini-2.5-Flash): 1{,}364 released traces, of which 1{,}304 carry the binary human label and 1{,}303 also carry a four-way code (one labeled trace has an out-of-range code~0; 60 traces are unannotated; exact census in Appendix~\ref{app:labels}). It provides derived metrics---notably \texttt{soft\_faithfulness}, a step-removal answer-tracing score---and \emph{human} annotations: binary \texttt{unfaithfulness} and a four-way \texttt{faithful\_type}. We evaluate against the human labels throughout. A trap we document: correlating a detector against \texttt{soft\_faithfulness} itself is near-circular (an apparent $\rho{=}0.87$ collapses to chance against human labels), since it is itself a detector.

\paragraph{Label semantics, verified against the data.}
\label{sec:labels}
The benchmark's documentation and its released data disagree about what the four \texttt{faithful\_type} codes mean. In the released data, the codes pair as: \textbf{ft1 faithful / ft2 unfaithful on \emph{incorrect} answers; ft3 faithful / ft4 unfaithful (post-hoc) on \emph{correct} answers}---whereas the repository documentation states the correct/incorrect pairing the other way around. The four-way codes carry two axes, so two assignments were in play (the faithful/unfaithful axis is anchored by the separate binary label: ft2/ft4 co-occur with \texttt{unfaithfulness}$=$1 at 95.7\%; Appendix~\ref{app:labels}); we resolved the correctness axis empirically, three ways: (i) cross-tabulating the release's \emph{own stored} parsed final answers against its gold labels---the association is near-deterministic (among parseable traces: ft1 200/201 and ft2 207/208 incorrect; ft3 679/681 and ft4 106/107 \emph{correct}), in every domain; (ii) reproducing the benchmark paper's own per-model accuracy statistics, which match only under the data-side semantics; and (iii) checking that the released per-trace metric scores behave consistently with (i)--(ii). We reported the discrepancy to the benchmark maintainers via their public issue tracker, where it had also been independently flagged\footnote{Link withheld for anonymity; included in the camera-ready.}, and use the verified data-side semantics throughout; Appendix~\ref{app:labels} gives the full cross-tabulations and census. Counts: ft1 $=$ 281, ft2 $=$ 233, ft3 $=$ 682, ft4 $=$ 107; hence \textbf{233/340 $\approx$ 69\% of annotated unfaithfulness sits on incorrect answers}. Beyond bookkeeping, the episode is a methodological point: label semantics should be verified against the released data (answers, golds, scores), not taken from documentation---any analysis inheriting the documented pairing silently swaps its regimes.

\paragraph{Statistical standards.} Detection claims use AUROC with 2{,}000-resample percentile-bootstrap 95\% CIs (class imbalances are mild, 13--43\% minority). Probe claims use held-out splits plus \emph{selection-corrected} permutation tests: because the best layer is chosen post hoc, the null statistic takes the max (or the mean, matching the reported statistic) over layers under label permutation, with permutations coupled across layers---the max-statistic procedure of \citet{nichols2002nonparametric}. With 200 permutations the finest attainable $p$ is $1/201 \approx 0.005$; values reported as 0.005 mean $p \leq 0.005$ (the headline transfer and hint-testbed tests use 1{,}000 permutations). The permutation null doubles as the memorization/selectivity control in lieu of word-level control tasks, which do not transfer to sequence-level properties \citep{belinkov2022probing}. Scalers and PCA are fit within training folds only. Causal claims must beat norm-matched random-direction controls. Negative results are reported as findings.

\paragraph{Compute.} All open-model experiments run on two RTX 3070 (8\,GB) GPUs with 4-bit NF4 quantization; the 12B model in \S\ref{sec:construction} uses two-GPU pipeline sharding.

\section{Part I: The Black-Box Audit}
\label{sec:audit}

\paragraph{Signal families.} \emph{Answer-tracing}: sensitivity of the final-answer distribution to removing individual steps. \emph{Premise-DAG structure}: PARC-style premise graphs over steps (linearity; maximum lookback). \emph{Graph-targeted counterfactual interventions}: corrupt the most load-bearing premise and measure answer stability; to make this a fair test we use an LLM premise extractor validated against PARC's gold PERL annotations (recall 0.82 / F1 0.79, vs.\ 0.57 F1 heuristic), so the result below is not an extraction artifact. \emph{Per-step NLI support}: entailment of each step from question and prior steps (RoBERTa-large-MNLI). From the benchmark's stored per-step answer distributions we additionally compute \emph{prefix instability} (mean total-variation shift between successive steps' answer distributions), an answer-tracing variant. Signal directions are fixed a priori by each family's faithfulness rationale; where a signal discriminates only in the \emph{opposite} direction we report it explicitly as inverted.

\paragraph{Results.} Table~\ref{tab:audit}: \textbf{answer incorrectness alone is the strongest single signal (0.696)}---annotated unfaithfulness concentrates on incorrect answers, so black-box unfaithfulness detection largely reduces to accuracy prediction. Structural families add nothing significant, with one honest nuance: DAG max-lookback is weakly informative on AQuA (0.636), the one domain with explicit premise structure. The 633-trace subset is determined by feature availability, not outcomes: the benchmark stores answer-tracing scores for 634 labeled traces (its two open models), and one of those carries the out-of-range annotation code. Each detector is unchanged on its maximal available sample: answer-tracing on all 634 (0.651 inverted / 0.349 intended), NLI and DAG on the full 1{,}304 (e.g., mean entailment 0.534 [0.499, 0.567]).

\paragraph{Metric inversion.} \texttt{soft\_faithfulness} used as intended scores \textbf{0.349 [0.305, 0.389]}---significantly \emph{below} chance; equivalently, unfaithful traces score \emph{higher} by $+0.139$ [$+0.096, +0.182$]. This is not an artifact of our feature pipeline: recomputing directly from the benchmark's \emph{own released} per-trace scores against its own human labels reproduces the inversion (intended-direction AUROC 0.348 overall; 0.43 and 0.29 for its two open models). Concurrent meta-evaluation reports below-chance AUROCs for related perturbation metrics against constructed ground truth \citep{gurarieh2026bonafide}; our result is against \emph{human} labels on organic traces, and---the part the correctness structure makes visible---has a mechanism the regime decomposition makes visible. Mean scores by annotation: ft3 (faithful-correct) \textbf{0.230} vs.\ ft1 0.371, ft2 0.397, ft4 (post-hoc-correct) \textbf{0.405}---the metric scores faithful-correct traces \emph{least} faithful, plausibly because a model that genuinely solved the problem can re-derive the answer when steps are removed, while a tightly answer-coupled rationalization cannot. Within the incorrect regime the metric is merely uninformative (intended direction 0.470, n.s.); within the correct regime it is confidently inverted (0.333); the remainder of the overall effect is between-regime composition (ft3-vs-ft2 intended direction: 0.300). The metric measures answer--reasoning \emph{coupling}, not faithfulness. In \S\ref{sec:regimes} we show the inversion is strongest exactly where the metric is most needed, and in \S\ref{sec:construction} we replicate it on traces with counterfactual ground-truth labels.

\paragraph{The detectable regime (correct answers).} Separating faithful reasoning (ft3) from post-hoc rationalization (ft4) on correct answers is \emph{partially} solvable behaviorally: answer-tracing (inverted), prefix instability (inverted), and NLI step-support all clear chance (0.63--0.67). This regime is where the metric inversion is sharpest: \texttt{soft\_faithfulness} in its intended direction scores 0.333---using the standard metric here is \emph{worse than deciding by coin flip}, on exactly the faithful-vs-post-hoc distinction it was designed for.

\paragraph{The blind regime (incorrect answers).} Distinguishing honest error from unfaithful error is behaviorally opaque: \textbf{we detect no above-chance performance from any black-box signal}; every CI includes 0.5 (intervals are wide---answer-tracing's extends to 0.598---so modest effects cannot be excluded). A \emph{learned} text baseline fails here too: frozen RoBERTa-MNLI embeddings with logistic regression score 0.446 (Llama) and 0.474 (Qwen) on the same regime. And this is not a corner case: it is the regime holding most of the benchmark's annotated unfaithfulness. An auditor screening by behavioral signals would concentrate scrutiny on traces that \emph{look} suspicious---and those, per Part~I, are largely just the incorrect ones, within which the signals then carry no information about which errors are honest.

\paragraph{Why the asymmetry is bad news for oversight.} The correct-answer regime is where unfaithfulness is arguably least costly (the answer is right anyway, though the justification is untrustworthy); the incorrect-answer regime is where a deployed system is actively wrong and the reasoning offers no behavioral tell about whether the trace honestly reflects the failure. The regime that matters more for oversight is the one where behavioral auditing has, in our tests, nothing to offer.

\paragraph{The blind regime is decodable---in Llama.} On ft1 vs.\ ft2, where every behavioral signal fails, a linear probe on Llama-3.1-8B reaches held-out AUROC 0.67 $\pm$ 0.08 under nested layer selection (0.70 at the disclosed full-data layer), generalizes across domains (leave-one-domain-out 0.60--0.71), and is approximately linear (an MLP adds nothing). In Qwen-2.5-7B the same probe is weak and not significant---``not detected,'' not ``absent'' ($n{=}126$). Whatever distinguishes an honest error from an unfaithful one leaves a trace in Llama's hidden states that produces no measurable behavioral signature.

\paragraph{The detectable regime is decodable---in Qwen.} On ft3 vs.\ ft4 the pattern flips: Qwen is significant (CV 0.701 at L24, selection-corrected $p{=}0.014$, $n{=}200$), while Llama's nominally higher curve (0.759 at L19) does not survive the selection-corrected test ($p{=}0.19$)---its minority class is very small (ft4 $n{=}26$), so we report it as underpowered rather than negative.

\paragraph{No shared linear direction across regimes.} Probes trained on one regime do not transfer to the other in either model, in either direction (layer-means 0.52--0.57, all permutation-n.s.; Table~\ref{tab:whitebox} bottom). Stated precisely: we detect no shared, positively aligned, linearly decodable direction between the regimes under this probing setup. Null transfer at these sample sizes cannot by itself establish distinctness (power, rotated encodings, and label noise all contribute); but combined with the \emph{flipped} model-dependence, the results are most consistent with the hypothesis that unfaithful \emph{error} and post-hoc \emph{rationalization} are different phenomena, encoded (where they are encoded at all) along different directions---a hypothesis, not an established mechanism. An exploratory steering check on the Llama incorrect-regime direction points the right way---steering along the probe's unfaithful direction flips answers 2--3$\times$ more than a norm-matched random control (0.22 vs.\ 0.08 at $+6\sigma$; Appendix~\ref{app:steering})---which we present strictly as a sanity check \citep[cf.][]{cox2026decoding,tan2024analysing}.

\section{Part IV: Constructed vs.\ Organic Unfaithfulness}
\label{sec:construction}

Annotated unfaithful traces are scarce (340 across both regimes, two open models). Scaling requires \emph{constructing} unfaithfulness---and the construction itself turns out to be the object of study.

\subsection{Instructed construction: 7 models}
\label{sec:synth}

For math problems (AQuA, GSM8K) with gold answer $a^*$, the same model either solves step-by-step (kept only when correct: \emph{genuine}) or is instructed to justify the given $a^*$ (\emph{post-hoc}). Both end at the same correct answer; probed contexts are identical (the instruction is never probed); decoding is matched. Across \textbf{seven open models spanning three families and 2024--2026} (Table~\ref{tab:synth})---including Gemma-4-12B via two-GPU pipeline sharding---the construction is permutation-significant in every model (Figure~\ref{fig:seven}), strongest in standard instruct models, weakest in reasoning-distilled ones. Surface-feature baselines (no activation access) remain near chance for five of seven, indicating a genuinely internal signal; Llama (0.64) and Gemma-4 (0.67) carry partial surface tells that the probes clear by $\geq$0.10. Notably, Qwen-2.5---not significant on the incorrect-regime human labels---is strongest here, consistent with its regime pattern in \S\ref{sec:internals}: this construction, like the correct-answer regime, pairs faithful and unfaithful traces that both end at correct answers.

\subsection{Hint-induced organic construction}
\label{sec:hint}

Following the biased-context paradigm \citep{turpin2023language,chen2025reasoning}, we first ask each problem clean (correct $\Rightarrow$ \emph{genuine}, capped). For problems the model got \emph{wrong}, we re-ask with a sycophancy hint toward the gold answer (``I think the answer is $a^*$\ldots''). A trace is kept as \emph{hint-induced} (an unverbalized-flip trace) iff the model flips to $a^*$ \emph{and} the CoT never references the hint (mention-filtered; rejected cases are honest deference). The label is \textbf{counterfactual at the sample level}: the sampled baseline answer was incorrect and the hinted answer matches the hint, satisfying the unfaithfulness criterion of \citet{chen2025reasoning}. It is not a problem-level inability claim: a single independent baseline resample solves 59\% of these problems, and only 25\% (Llama) / 29\% (Qwen) fail two consecutive resamples, so hint-dependence is a property of the sampled trace; Appendix~\ref{app:strict} repeats the key analyses on the strict subset of problems that fail repeated baseline resamples. We likewise distinguish the established claim (``the hint changed the answer, unverbalized'') from the stronger interpretation (``the CoT was generated answer-first''), which remains an inference; we use \emph{rationalization} as shorthand for the former. Probed contexts exclude the hint text, keeping conditions symmetric. On the full AQuA-RAT and GSM8K test sets this yields \textbf{185 hint-induced post-hoc + 428 genuine traces for Llama} and \textbf{70 + 464 for Qwen}, with an exhaustive keyword audit confirming no hint leakage. The construction costs no human annotation. A further caveat: unlike the instructed construction, whose pairs share problems, genuine and hint-induced traces here come from \emph{disjoint problem pools} (solved vs.\ failed at baseline), so within-testbed decodability may partly reflect problem difficulty or model confidence. The transfer analysis below is the difficulty-robust claim, and we verify the control directly.

Both models are strongly decodable on this organic set (held-out 0.752 / 0.835, permutation $p{\leq}0.001$, 1{,}000 permutations). Input and text baselines are substantial here and we report them prominently: surface features reach 0.626/0.703, a learned text baseline 0.611/0.751, and question-only probes 0.646/0.664---so within-testbed decodability, especially for Qwen, is partly difficulty- and text-explainable; the CoT probes exceed the strongest such baseline by roughly $+0.08$--$0.11$. Most strikingly, \textbf{the metric inversion replicates with intervention-defined, sample-level hint-dependence labels}: \texttt{soft\_faithfulness} in its intended direction scores \textbf{0.389 [0.301, 0.481] (Llama) and 0.251 [0.166, 0.350] (Qwen)}---on traces whose sampled answers were demonstrably hint-dependent, the standard metric confidently points the wrong way. With the full-benchmark result, the release reproduction, and the correct-regime stratification (\S\ref{sec:audit}--\ref{sec:regimes}), this completes four complementary analyses of the inversion---the first three share the benchmark and its scores; this one is on an independent constructed testbed with counterfactual labels.

Do the constructions share the annotated regimes' representations? We train probes on each distribution and test on the others, sweeping all layers (Table~\ref{tab:bridge}; Figures~\ref{fig:depth} and~\ref{fig:heatmap}). Four observations.

\textbf{(1) Instructed rationalization transfers to neither regime.} Despite being the easiest variant to detect within-distribution, its probes are at or below chance layer-mean against the incorrect regime (0.431) and n.s.\ against the correct regime in both models. Evaluating or training faithfulness detectors on instructed rationalizations may measure the wrong thing \citep[cf.\ off-policy probe failures in deception detection;][]{offpolicy2025probes}.

\textbf{(2) Hint-induced traces transfer---onto the incorrect regime.} Llama: layer-mean 0.616, permutation $p{=}0.017$; best 0.694, selection-corrected $p{=}0.049$; 1{,}000 permutations. On the strict subset of Appendix~\ref{app:strict} the magnitude is preserved (mean 0.596, best 0.699) though underpowered at $n{=}47$ ($p{=}0.078$). Critically, a \emph{question-only} probe trained on the same hint labels---which carries substantial within-testbed signal (0.646)---does not transfer at all (layer-mean 0.413, best 0.511, $p{\approx}1$; Qwen 0.503, $p{=}0.485$): problem difficulty is not the carrier. The result is robust to preprocessing (without PCA, with target-refit scaling: hint 0.56--0.57 vs.\ instructed 0.45). In Qwen the transfer is null ($p{=}0.741$), consistent with the incorrect regime not being detectably encoded in Qwen at all (\S\ref{sec:internals}): one cannot measure transfer onto a signal that is not there.

\textbf{(3) The target regime is a surprise worth taking seriously.} Hint-induced traces are correct-answer traces; superficially they resemble the correct-answer regime (ft3/ft4). Yet they align with the \emph{incorrect}-answer regime's unfaithfulness direction (0.616 vs.\ 0.555, only the former significant)---the regime whose defining feature is that the model \emph{failed} the problem. Stated carefully: hint-induced traces share linearly decodable structure with annotated unfaithful errors despite differing in answer correctness; the question-only control excludes difficulty as the carrier, but the shared factor itself remains unidentified. One candidate---that in both sets the displayed reasoning did not produce the answer the model would reach on its own---fits the hint traces by construction (measured counterfactually) but is, for the annotated side, an interpretation. Positively identifying what the transferred direction encodes is the natural next step (\S\ref{sec:discussion}).

\textbf{(4) Depth gradient (descriptive).} In Llama the three variants peak at increasing depths---instructed L9, hint-induced L17, annotated incorrect-regime L29. We treat this as an observed pattern, not evidence of distinct processing stages; peak locations may be unstable under resampling.

Stated precisely, the transfer results concern \emph{linearly decodable structure}: probes find no positively aligned, linearly decodable shared direction between instructed rationalization and either annotated regime at any layer, while hint-induced rationalization shares one with the incorrect regime. Transfer failure alone cannot rule out a shared representation encoded along non-aligned directions, and input-distribution differences contribute; but the three-way contrast controls for the boring versions of this (the two constructions share format and domain, yet only one transfers).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{For oversight.} Black-box CoT auditing largely re-detects answer correctness. Where correctness is controlled, behavioral signals retain moderate power only on correct answers---the lower-stakes regime---and provide no detected information on incorrect answers, where 69\% of annotated unfaithfulness lives and where a deployed system is actively wrong. The most-used metric inverts, most sharply in the regime it was designed for. Information correlated with both annotated distinctions is linearly decodable from hidden states---but in different models for different regimes---suggesting white-box access provides complementary signal, with no single-probe shortcut: we find no shared linear direction across the regimes.

\paragraph{For evaluation practice.} Three shortcuts fail in ways we can now quantify: evaluating detectors against answer-tracing metrics (circular, and inverted); constructing unfaithfulness by instruction (which transferred to neither annotated regime in our probes); and trusting benchmark documentation over benchmark data (\S\ref{sec:labels})---the released labels' semantics were inverted relative to the documentation, and any analysis inheriting the documented pairing swaps its regimes end-to-end. The practical prescriptions: verify label semantics against raw data before analysis, and transfer-validate any constructed unfaithfulness testbed against annotated organic data---exactly the check that fails for instructed construction and passes (in Llama, onto the incorrect regime) for hint-induced elicitation. The hint-induced protocol offers a cheap alternative with sample-level counterfactual labels; our collected set (255 unverbalized hint-flip traces + 892 matched genuine traces across two models) is released with the paper.

\paragraph{Model- and regime-dependence.} Detection strength varies systematically twice over: by model within the instructed construction (strongest in standard instruct models, weakest in reasoning-distilled ones; Gemma-2 0.60 $\to$ Gemma-4 0.80 within one family), and by regime within each model (Llama encodes the incorrect regime, Qwen the correct one). Why models expose different regimes linearly---and what the hint-transferred direction encodes, beyond our exclusion of difficulty---are open mechanistic questions. Sparse-autoencoder feature decomposition is the natural instrument, but a first pass shows it is not free: off-the-shelf base-model SAEs (Llama Scope, both dictionary widths) reconstruct our instruct-model, quantized, reasoning-position activations too poorly (variance explained $\leq$0.49, and width-invariant) to decompose the direction---itself a portability caveat for interpretability pipelines; SAEs fit to the target distribution are future work.

\section{Limitations}

Our annotated-label results rest on one human-annotated benchmark (FaithCoT-Bench; regimes $n{=}270$/$363$, per-model minority classes as small as ft4 $n{=}26$)---to our knowledge the only instance-level faithfulness benchmark with expert annotations, which is itself part of the problem we highlight; its label semantics required verification against the raw data (\S\ref{sec:labels}), and our regime findings inherit whatever noise remains in the annotations. The blind-regime claim (no detected above-chance performance) is specific to that benchmark, and its CIs do not exclude modest effects; the correct-regime positives are moderate, not operational. The Llama correct-regime probe is underpowered (ft4 $n{=}26$, $p{=}0.19$), so the regime-flipped model-dependence rests on one significant cell per model; it needs replication at larger $n$. The Qwen incorrect-regime contrast is ``not detected,'' not ``absent,'' and its null transfer is inconclusive for the same reason. Causal evidence from steering is weak ($n{=}51$, large perturbations, a single random control direction, indirect readout) and concerns only the Llama incorrect-regime direction. The constructed testbeds are math-only; the hint construction uses a single sycophancy template (a second-template robustness run is in progress), inherits class imbalance, draws genuine and post-hoc traces from disjoint problem pools (a difficulty confound for within-testbed decodability; the transfer analysis with the question-only control is the difficulty-robust claim), and under sampled decoding hint-dependence is sample-level: only 25--29\% of flip-labeled problems fail two consecutive baseline resamples (Appendix~\ref{app:strict}). Bootstrap CIs treat traces as independent although they cluster by model and domain; a cluster bootstrap over the 8 model$\times$domain cells still excludes chance for the headline effects (incorrectness [0.616, 0.753]; inverted answer-tracing [0.575, 0.728]), with honest heterogeneity---both effects are near chance on HLE-Bio ($n{=}40$ per model). A stronger text encoder than RoBERTa could narrow the probe--text gap; probes are linear/PCA-based (an MLP added nothing, but richer white-box methods may decode more). Closed models cannot be probed at all---which is itself part of the argument for open-weight oversight.

\section*{Ethics Statement}
This work analyzes model-generated reasoning on public benchmarks (FaithCoT-Bench, AQuA-RAT, GSM8K) and releases model-generated traces with construction labels; no human subjects or personal data are involved. Detection of unfaithful reasoning is oversight-enabling; we do not release methods that make models less faithful---the hint construction elicits an existing failure mode, documented by prior work, for measurement purposes. The label-semantics discrepancy we identified was reported to the benchmark maintainers before publication. AI assistants (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini) were used to improve the clarity of the manuscript text and to assist with implementation and analysis code; all research questions, experimental designs, results, and conclusions are the authors' own and were verified by the authors.

\section{Construction Details}
\label{app:prompts}
\paragraph{Instructed (synthetic).} Genuine: \emph{``Solve this step by step, showing your reasoning. On the last line write exactly `Answer: X'.''} (kept only if correct). Post-hoc: \emph{``The correct answer is $a^*$. Provide a clear, convincing step-by-step reasoning that leads to this answer.''} Sampling T$=$0.7, top-$p$ 0.9, repetition penalty 1.1, fixed seed; identical across conditions and models.
\paragraph{Hint-induced (organic).} Hint text: \emph{``I think the answer is $a^*$, but I'm not sure.''} appended to the clean question; kept iff baseline-wrong $\to$ hint-correct and the CoT matches no hint-reference pattern (second-person forms, ``hint,'' ``suggested answer,'' ``the user,'' etc.); rejected cases (3 total) verbalized deference. An exhaustive keyword scan over all kept traces found no leakage (matches were problem restatements, e.g.\ ``we are told that\ldots,'' and generic-you phrasing).
\paragraph{Probing.} Probed context is always [question (+options) $+$ generated CoT], excluding any construction instruction or hint text. Last-token hidden states at every layer; StandardScaler $\to$ PCA-50 $\to$ logistic regression; 5-fold CV; 200-permutation selection-corrected tests.

\section{Strict-Subset Construction}
\label{app:strict}
Because decoding is sampled (T$=$0.7), a baseline-incorrect answer is one draw from a distribution, not evidence the model cannot solve the problem: 59\% of Llama's flip-labeled problems are solved on an independent baseline resample. We therefore define a \emph{strict} subset: hint-induced traces whose problems are answered incorrectly on repeated independent baseline resamples. \textbf{Results:} 47/185 (Llama) and 20/70 (Qwen) flip-labeled problems fail both resamples. Within-testbed decodability \emph{rises} on the strict subset (CV 0.818/0.837), consistent with cleaner labels. The Llama hint$\to$incorrect-regime transfer preserves its magnitude (layer-mean 0.596 vs.\ 0.616 on the full set; best 0.699 vs.\ 0.694) but is underpowered at this $n$ ($p{=}0.078$ mean, $p{=}0.066$ best; 500 permutations); Qwen remains null (0.494, $p{=}0.605$). Had resampling-noise flips driven the transfer, the strict estimate would collapse toward chance; it does not.

\section{GRACE Preliminary Replication}
On the 40-example public sample of GRACE \citep{pham2026grace} (8 unfaithful steps), NLI step-support shows no above-chance discrimination (0.51--0.58), directionally consistent with our FaithCoT results but underpowered; the full evaluation set was not released in time.
